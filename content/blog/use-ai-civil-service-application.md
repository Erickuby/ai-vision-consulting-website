# How to Use AI in a Civil Service Application Without Losing Your Voice

You can use AI to organise your evidence, understand a vacancy and improve clarity, but the final Civil Service application must remain truthful, personal and based on your own experience. Treat AI as an editor and thinking partner, not as the author of achievements you cannot defend. Read the vacancy instructions and the official Civil Service guidance before using any tool, because requirements can differ by campaign. ## Quick answer

Use AI for permitted support tasks such as turning a job description into a checklist, asking questions that help you remember relevant evidence, testing whether an example is clear and cutting unnecessary words. Do not let it invent events, responsibilities, figures or outcomes. Civil Service guidance says not to use AI during ability or personality assessments, or to answer questions during live or pre-recorded interviews. Do not submit generic text that could belong to anyone.

Start with your own notes. Check the vacancy against the official [Civil Service guidance on artificial intelligence and recruitment](https://www.civil-service-careers.gov.uk/artificial-intelligence-and-recruitment/) and the published [Success Profiles](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/success-profiles). Then use AI to review, not replace, your judgement. You should understand and be able to explain every sentence you submit.

## Key takeaways

- Follow the vacancy instructions and official Civil Service guidance ahead of any advice in this article.
- Gather genuine evidence before opening an AI tool.
- Match examples to the Success Profile elements named in the advert, rather than guessing what assessors want.
- Use AI to ask questions, identify gaps and improve readability where the campaign allows it, not to manufacture evidence.
- Do not use AI for ability or personality assessments, or for answers in live or pre-recorded interviews.
- Check privacy, accuracy and voice before submitting, and keep a final copy you can revisit before interview.

## What support may be acceptable?

The safest principle is simple: AI should help you express your experience, not create it. Official guidance and the instructions in the specific job advert take priority. If a campaign limits or prohibits a particular use, follow that requirement.

Where the vacancy permits it, reasonable support may include summarising an advert into a planning checklist, creating a table of criteria and evidence, suggesting recall questions, checking whether your actions are clear, shortening a draft without changing its meaning, and proofreading. The official guidance expressly says you should not use AI during ability or personality assessments or to answer questions during a live or pre-recorded interview.

Risky or inappropriate use includes asking AI to invent an example, exaggerate your role, add unsupported numbers or write an entire statement from little personal input. It is also risky to copy an answer without checking it. AI can produce confident errors and polished clichés. A sentence may sound impressive while misrepresenting what happened.

Never paste sensitive operational, personal or confidential information into a public AI service. Remove names, contact details, case information and anything covered by employer policy. Use neutral labels where identity is irrelevant.

## Understand Success Profiles before drafting

Civil Service recruitment may assess candidates through Success Profiles. Depending on the role, the advert may refer to Behaviours, Strengths, Ability, Experience or Technical elements. Not every campaign uses every element, and the advert should tell you what is being assessed.

Read the official Success Profiles documents rather than relying on an AI summary alone. Note the grade, named behaviours, essential criteria, word limits and any format instructions. A good response addresses the actual assessment criteria. A generic leadership story is less useful if the vacancy asks for evidence of making effective decisions in a particular context.

For behaviour examples, a structure such as Situation, Task, Action and Result can help. Give enough context to make the example understandable, then explain your own actions, reasoning and outcome clearly. There is no universal allocation of words that suits every example. The assessor needs a clear chain from challenge to action to outcome, not stiff labels.

## Gather evidence before asking AI to write anything

Begin with an evidence bank. Note relevant situations from work, volunteering, education, caring responsibilities or community activity. The useful number depends on the vacancy and the range of evidence you have, so favour relevance over a fixed total. Use factual notes rather than polished prose.

For each situation, record:

1. **Context:** What was happening, and what made it difficult?
2. **Responsibility:** What were you personally expected to achieve?
3. **Actions:** What did you decide, communicate, analyse or change?
4. **Reasoning:** Why did you take those steps?
5. **Result:** What improved, finished or became clearer?
6. **Verification:** Which details, dates or figures can you honestly support?
7. **Learning:** What would you repeat or adjust next time?

Separate team outcomes from your contribution. “We delivered the project” does not show what you did. Describe your own decisions and actions. Do not add a percentage because it looks persuasive. If no figure exists, give an observable outcome such as a deadline met or a process adopted.

## A step-by-step AI-assisted workflow

### 1. Read the advert without AI

Highlight the essential criteria, assessed Success Profile elements, required documents, word limits and deadline. Note any statement about AI use. This first reading prevents a summary tool from filtering out an important instruction.

### 2. Build a requirement-to-evidence table

Create two columns: requirement and possible example. Add a third for gaps. You can ask AI to turn the advert into a checklist, but compare that checklist with the original line by line.

### 3. Choose the strongest genuine example

Select evidence based on relevance, your personal contribution and a clear outcome. Do not choose the most senior-sounding story by default. A small, well-explained decision can show more than a large project described vaguely.

### 4. Draft from memory and records

Write a rough version in your normal language. Include concrete actions, people you considered, options you weighed and the result. This gives the draft your natural rhythm before AI starts suggesting polished phrases.

### 5. Ask AI to diagnose, not rewrite

Request a gap review. Ask where the example becomes unclear, where “we” hides your contribution, which claims need evidence and what a sceptical reader might question. Keep the advert criteria in the prompt, but remove sensitive information.

### 6. Revise one issue at a time

Fix weak evidence yourself. Ask for a shorter sentence or clearer order if needed, but avoid a wholesale rewrite. Plain English is usually more credible than familiar generated phrases.

### 7. Run an authenticity check

Read the statement aloud. Replace words you would never use. Confirm that job titles, dates, systems, decisions and results are accurate. Ask whether a former colleague would recognise the event and your role in it.

### 8. Check compliance and save your final version

Return to the advert and official guidance. Confirm the word count and format. Keep the submitted version for interview preparation and be ready to discuss every example.

## A useful review prompt

Use only anonymised material and adapt the bracketed sections:

> Act as a careful editor, not a ghostwriter. Review my draft against the criteria below. Do not invent achievements, numbers, responsibilities or context. First, list: (1) where my personal actions are unclear, (2) claims that need evidence, (3) parts that do not address the criteria, (4) vague or generic wording, and (5) possible cuts. Then ask me up to five factual questions that would help me improve the draft myself. Preserve my straightforward UK English and do not rewrite the full answer. Criteria: [paste relevant criteria]. Draft: [paste anonymised draft].

Verify every observation. AI feedback is a second opinion, not an assessment decision.

## Limits and risks to manage

**Generic answers:** Repeating the advert proves little. Show decisions and actions.

**False detail:** Delete anything you did not supply and cannot verify.

**Loss of voice:** Retain your normal vocabulary and sentence length.

**Privacy:** Do not expose personal data, security details or confidential workplace information. Follow the policies that apply to you.

**Poor preparation:** Your application should record experience you understand and can discuss at interview.

**Changing requirements:** Recruitment instructions can vary. Always check the current advert and official Civil Service sources at the point of application.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can AI write my Civil Service personal statement?

AI can help you plan and review a personal statement, but asking it to produce the substance creates serious authenticity and accuracy risks. Start with your own evidence and wording. Follow the specific advert and official Civil Service guidance, then use AI for limited tasks such as identifying gaps, improving clarity or reducing repetition.

### Is it acceptable to use AI for spelling and grammar?

The official [Civil Service guidance on artificial intelligence and recruitment](https://www.civil-service-careers.gov.uk/artificial-intelligence-and-recruitment/) gives examples of acceptable support, including checking spelling and grammar, while making clear that information must be accurate, truthful and your own work. Review every edit because a grammar tool can alter meaning, replace precise terms or make your voice unnaturally formal. The vacancy instructions remain decisive.

### How do I stop an AI-edited answer sounding generic?

Draft first in your own words and retain concrete details about your decisions, reasoning and contribution. Ask for diagnosis rather than a full rewrite. Read the result aloud and remove phrases you would not say. If a sentence could fit thousands of applicants, replace it with accurate evidence from your situation.

### Can I paste a job description into an AI tool?

You may use publicly available advert text for planning, subject to the tool's terms and any recruitment instructions. Do not assume the generated summary is complete. Compare it with the original advert and official Success Profiles. Avoid pasting private correspondence, assessment content or personal information that is not meant for public processing.

### Will using AI improve my chance of getting the job?

No tool can guarantee selection. AI may help you organise evidence or notice unclear writing, but assessors judge the application against the campaign's criteria. Relevant experience, honest examples and compliance with instructions matter. Use AI carefully, verify its output and prepare to explain every claim in your own words.
